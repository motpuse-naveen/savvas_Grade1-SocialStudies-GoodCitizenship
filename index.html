In This Text Set

This text set includes five informational texts. All help explain an aspect of good citizenship.

How to Be a Good Citizen is the anchor text for this set. Depending on the needs of your class, you may choose to assign How to Be a Good Citizen for independent reading, or you may choose to read it aloud with the class. Beginning with the anchor text will ground students in concepts and academic vocabulary that are key to discussing the Big Question. As students read the other texts in the set, they will compare their new understandings, as well as the structure and features of the text, with the anchor text.

After students read the anchor text, you may choose to assign specific texts or encourage students to choose the texts that appeal to them the most. Use the table below to see where specific text structures and text features appear across the texts in this set.

Standards and Objectives

  • Social Studies Standard
    Explain how all people, not just official leaders, play important roles in a community.

  • Informational Text Focus
    In this text set, students will read informational texts to understand how descriptive, compare and contrast, cause and effect, and problem and solution text structures help explain ideas. They will learn how text features such as photographs, headings, and captions help their understanding as they read.

  • Content Objective
    Students will understand what it means to be a citizen and how people show good citizenship.

  • Language Objective
    Students will describe traits and examples of good citizens.

How to Be a Good Citizen

adapted from myWorld
Informational Text - Word Count: 225   Lexile: 480L

Instructional Focus

  • Social Studies Standard Explain how all people, not just official leaders, play important roles in a community.

  • Content Objective Students will understand what it means to be a good citizen.

  • Language Objective Students will describe examples of good citizenship.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a descriptive text structure describes things and ideas.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will understand how photographs help to illustrate an idea.

Summary

The text describes traits of good citizens and explains how citizens help their communities.

Before Reading

Big Question
Remind students of the Big Question: What does it mean to be a good citizen? Say: Good citizens follow rules and laws. They help to make their community better. How can you be a good citizen?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words rights, responsibilities, and community. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Say: A right is something you are allowed to do. For example, kids have the right to go to school. What are some other rights that people have? A responsibility is something you should do. You have a responsibility to get ready for school in the morning. What are some other responsibilities you have? Explain that a community can be a group of people who live in an area. Ask: What community are you part of?

Oral Vocabulary
Some of the vocabulary in How to Be a Good Citizen includes words that might be new or challenging. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: When people volunteer, they offer to help other people. Some volunteers might collect or pick up cans to recycle. What is another way to volunteer?

WRITE/DRAW/DEFINE

Share a three-column chart with students. Label the left column “Write,” the middle column “Draw,” and the right column “Define.” Have students copy the chart on a piece of paper. Help students write the vocabulary words citizen, right, and responsibility in the left column. As they read, help them draw a picture and add a definition for each term. An entry for citizen might look like this:

Write Draw Define
citizen a member of a community

During Reading

Elements Of Informational Text

Text Structure Explain that How to Be a Good Citizen is a descriptive text. Say: When you describe something, you explain what it is. You use details or examples. Project the text, or direct students to screen 4 (Printable Text p. x). Say: This part of the text helps describe what a good citizen is. A good citizen is someone who follows rules and laws. Direct students to screen 5 (Printable Text p. x) and ask students to find another detail about good citizens.

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Descriptive Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about descriptive structure and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “explains what something is” and “gives details”

Text Features Say: Pictures can also help explain ideas. Direct students to screen 3 (Printable Text p. x). Ask: What do these pictures show? Read the text on the screen. Explain that one picture shows a right, and the other picture shows a responsibility. Say: Citizens have rights and responsibilities. How do the pictures help you understand this idea?

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column write “Pictures.” Work with the class to fill in the column with examples of how pictures help readers understand the text. For example: “help readers picture or see the writer’s ideas”

After Reading

Vocabulary

After students have read How to Be a Good Citizen, have them review their Write/Draw/Define charts. Ask: Is there anything you want to add to the chart?

Display images from How to Be a Good Citizen to help students review the words citizen, responsibility, and right.

Discuss Across Texts

Encourage students to make connections between How to Be a Good Citizen and other texts they have read on related subjects. You may wish to provide sentence frames to scaffold discussion.

  • In this text, you learned that good citizens help other people. What else have you read about people who help others?
  • Can you think of a story about a character who is a good citizen? How does the character show good citizenship?
  • In this text, you read about the rights citizens have. What else have you read about people’s rights? Why is it important to have rights?

Track the Texts After the discussion, have students return to the Good Citizenship: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in the How to Be a Good Citizen row with something they learned about how a good citizen behaves.

Discuss The Big Question

Lead a class discussion around this text’s Big Question: What does it mean to be a good citizen? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • How can good citizens help people in their neighborhood?
  • In the text, good citizens follow the rules and laws. Why is it important to follow the rules?
  • How can you be a good citizen when you play a game? Why is it important to play fair?

Write About The Big Question

Have students draw a picture of one thing that good citizens do. Ask them to write a sentence to describe the picture.

Being a Good Citizen at School

adapted from myWorld
Informational Text - Word Count: 226   Lexile: 470L

Instructional Focus

  • Social Studies Standard Explain how all people, not just official leaders, play important roles in a community.

  • Content Objective Students will understand how they can show good citizenship at school.

  • Language Objective Students will name and describe traits of good citizens.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a descriptive text structure helps explain something, using details and examples.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will understand how headings help to organize a text and make it easier for readers to find information.

Summary

The text explains how children can be good citizens at school and how their actions can affect the school community.

Before Reading

Big Question
Remind students of the Big Question: What does it mean to be a good citizen? Ask: What are some things good citizens do? How can you be a good citizen at school?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words respect, kind, share, and rules. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Ask: Who is a person you respect? A kind person helps others. What is something you do when you are being kind? Why would a kind person share? A rule tells what to do or what not to do. Can you give me an example of one of our classroom rules?

Oral Vocabulary
Some of the vocabulary in How to Be a Good Citizen includes words that might be new or challenging. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: You can respect others by showing that you care about how they feel. How else can you show respect?

WRITE/DRAW/DEFINE

Share a three-column chart with columns labeled “Write,” “Draw,” and “Define.” Have students create their own chart in a notebook. Help kids write new words under “Write,” and draw and define them based on details in the text. An entry for kind might look like this:

Write Draw Define
kind helpful

During Reading

Elements Of Informational Text

Text Structure Say: This text uses a descriptive text structure. Writers describe something by telling what it is like. This text describes what good citizens do at school. Have students preview screen 2 (Printable Text p. x). Ask: How would you describe a good citizen? How can you be a good citizen at school?

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Descriptive Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about descriptive structure, and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “tells what something is like”

Text Features Project the text or direct students to screen 4 (Printable Text p. x). Say: Do you notice how the words “Follow the Rules” are written in bigger, darker letters than the other words? “Follow the Rules” is a heading. Writers use headings to show what a section, or part, of the text is about. Ask students what they think they will read about in this part of the text.

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column write “Headings.” Work with the class to fill in the column with examples of how headings help readers understand the text. For example: “tell what the text is about”

After Reading

Vocabulary

After students have read Being a Good Citizen at School, have them review their Write/Draw/Define charts. Ask: Are there any other words you’d like to add?

Display images from Being a Good Citizen at School to help students review the meaning of community, kind, and rules.

Discuss Across Texts

Encourage students to make connections between Being a Good Citizen at School and other texts they have read on related subjects. You may wish to provide sentence frames to scaffold discussion.

  • What else have you read about being a good citizen? What do good citizens do?
  • Being a Good Citizen at School describes rules for being a good sport. What else have you read about being a good sport and playing fair?
  • In How to Be a Good Citizen, you learned that citizens have rights and responsibilities. What rights do you have at school? What responsibilities do you have at school?

Track the Texts After the discussion, have students return to the Good Citizenship: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in the Being a Good Citizen at School row with something they learned about being a good citizen at school.

Discuss The Big Question

Lead a class discussion around this text’s Big Question: What does it mean to be a good citizen? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • In the text, you learned about rules for speaking and listening. Why is it important to listen to other people?
  • How does being a good citizen make your school a better place?
  • Is it hard to be a good citizen at school? Why or why not?

Write About The Big Question

Remind students that good citizens follow the rules. Ask them to write a school rule and draw a picture to illustrate their rule. Have students share why they think the rule will help people be good citizens.

Kids Who Help Their Communities

by Nella Barker
Informational Text - Word Count: 244   Lexile: 470L

Instructional Focus

  • Social Studies Standard Explain how all people, not just official leaders, play important roles in a community.

  • Content Objective Students will learn how kids demonstrate good citizenship in their communities.

  • Language Objective Students will describe actions they could take to help their school or neighborhood.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a problem and solution structure can show how different problems are solved.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will understand how headings help organize information in a text.

Summary

The text describes examples of real kids who have led efforts to help their community or the world.

Before Reading

Big Question
Remind students of the Big Question: What does it mean to be a good citizen? Ask: How do you think Kids Who Help Their Communities will help you think about the Big Question? What do good citizens do for their communities?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words citizen, citizenship, and kind. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Ask: Who is a citizen in a community? Who do you think is an example of good citizenship? What would be a kind thing to do when a friend is sad?

Oral Vocabulary
Some of the vocabulary in Kids Who Help Their Communities includes words that might be new or challenging. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: People can donate, or give, things to help other people. In this text, one kid asks people to donate food. What are some other things people could donate?

WRITE/DRAW/DEFINE

Share a three-column chart with columns labeled “Write,” “Draw,” and “Define.” Have students create their own chart in a notebook. Help kids write new words under “Write,” and draw and define them based on details in the text. An entry for community might look like this:

Write Draw Define
community group of people who live in a place or have something in common

During Reading

Elements of Informational Text

Text Structure Say: This is a problem and solution text. A problem is something that needs to be fixed. A solution fixes the problem. Direct students’ attention to the “William” section of the text. Say: William worried that people didn’t have enough to eat. He asked people to give or donate food. The problem is that people didn’t have enough food. The solution is that William asked people to donate food. Guide students to find other problems and solutions in the text.

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Problem and Solution Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about problem and solution structure, and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: "tells how a problem is fixed"

Text Features Have students preview the headings in the text. Say: This is a text about kids who help others. The first heading is “William.” The second heading is “Ella.” What do you notice about these headings? Guide students to understand that the headings are all kids’ names. Tell them that the headings show what they will read about.

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column write “Headings.” Work with the class to fill in the column with examples of how headings help readers interact with the text. For example: “help the reader predict what they will read about”

After Reading

Vocabulary

After students have read Kids Who Help Their Communities, have them review their Write/Draw/Define charts. Ask: Are there any words you’d like to add?

Display images from Kids Who Help Their Communities to help students review vocabulary words, such as donate, community, and kind.

Discuss Across Texts

Encourage students to make connections between Kids Who Help Their Communities and other texts they have read on related subjects. You may wish to provide sentence frames to scaffold your discussion.

  • What else have you read about people who help their communities?
  • In Kids Who Help Their Communities, kids solve problems in their communities. What other texts have you read about a problem in a community? How did people solve it?
  • In How to Be a Good Citizen, you learned about what it means to be a good citizen. What do good citizens do? How are these kids good citizens?

Track the Texts After the discussion, have students return to the Good Citizenship: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in the Kids Who Help Their Communities row with something they learned about how kids can be good citizens by helping others.

Discuss The Big Question

Lead a class discussion around this text’s Big Question: What does it mean to be a good citizen? You may wish to begin the discussion by sharing the ways you help your own community. As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about people who help?” or “What is another way to be a good citizen?”

  • Think about the kids you read about. How are they good citizens to their communities?
  • What community do you live in? How can you be a good citizen in your community?

Write About the Big Question

Have students think of two ways they can help their community. Ask them to draw a picture and write a sentence about each way to help.

Citizens in Our Community

by Lewis Kim
Informational Text - Word Count: 229   Lexile: 480L

Instructional Focus

  • Social Studies Standard Explain how all people, not just official leaders, play important roles in a community.

  • Content Objective Students will recognize how people in many different jobs help the community.

  • Language Objective Students will describe how a specific job can help the community.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a compare and contrast text structure shows how things are the same and different.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will understand how photographs can provide visual examples of information provided in a text.

Summary

The text explains many different jobs that adults can have that help their community.

Before Reading

Big Question

Remind students of the Big Question: What does it mean to be a good citizen? Ask: How do you think Citizens in Our Community will help you think about the Big Question? Who are some good citizens in your community?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words citizen, community, care, and laws. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Say: A citizen is a person who is part of a community. The community can be a neighborhood, a country, or another place. What is an example of a community? How can you be a good citizen in a community? How do laws or rules help people in a community? How do people care for us, or keep us healthy?

Oral Vocabulary

Much of the vocabulary in Citizens in Our Community should be familiar to most students, but other texts in this set include words that might be new or challenging. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: People have responsibilities, or things that they should do. What responsibilities does a firefighter have? What responsibilities does a doctor have?

WRITE/DRAW/DEFINE

Share a three-column chart with columns labeled “Write,” “Draw,” and “Define.” Have students create their own chart on a piece of paper. Help kids write new words under “Write,” and draw and define them based on details in the text. An entry for community might look like this:

Write Draw Define
community group of people living in a place

During Reading

Elements of Informational Text

Text Structure Say: This text has a compare and contrast structure. It shows how things are the same and different. Project the text or direct students to screen 3 (Printable Text page x). Say: Firefighters and paramedics have different jobs. Firefighters put out fires. Paramedics bring people to the hospital. What is one way these jobs are the same? (Possible response: Both help people.) Point out clue words for comparing and contrasting in the text, such as different and all.

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Compare and Contrast Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about compare and contrast structure, and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “tells how things are the same and different”

Text Features Say: Look at the photographs. What do they show? (Possible response: people working) Photographs can also help us understand how things are the same and different. Encourage students to discuss how the jobs shown in the photographs are the same and different.

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column write “Pictures.” Work with the class to fill in the column with examples of how pictures help readers understand the text. For example: “help readers compare different things”

After Reading

Vocabulary

After students have read Citizens in Our Community, have them review their Write/Draw/Define charts. Ask: Are there any words you want to add?

Display images from Citizens in Our Community to help students review vocabulary words such as community, responsible, and care.

Discuss Across Texts

Encourage students to make connections between Citizens in Our Community and other texts they have read on related subjects. You may wish to use sentence frames to scaffold discussion.

  • This text describes a firefighter, a teacher, and a mail carrier. What other facts have you learned about these jobs?
  • What else have you read about people helping their town or neighborhood? Why is it important for people to help their communities?
  • In How to Be a Good Citizen, you learned what good citizens do. How do those ideas help you understand this text?

Track the Texts After the discussion, have students return to the Good Citizenship: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in the Citizens in Our Community row with something they learned about how some jobs help the community.

Discuss The Big Question

Lead a class discussion around this text’s Big Question: What does it mean to be a good citizen? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • Think about the jobs you read about. Which one would you most like to have? How would you help your community if you had that job?
  • What are some other jobs that good citizens have? How do they help the community?

Write About the Big Question

Have students draw a picture of another job that good citizens do. Ask them to write 1-2 sentences about how the job helps people in the community.

Becoming a Citizen

by Jessica Holub
Informational Text - Word Count: 233   Lexile: 500L

Instructional Focus

  • Social Studies Standard Explain how all people, not just official leaders, play important roles in a community.

  • Content Objective Students will understand the rights and responsibilities of a citizen of the United States.

  • Language Objective Students will describe a fictional family’s experience becoming US citizens, as well as the rights and responsibilities that citizens have.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a cause and effect text structure shows why events happen.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will understand how headings can be used to organize a text and to locate specific information within a text.

Summary

The text explains the rights and responsibilities of American citizens, using the example of one particular family.

Before Reading

Big Question

Remind students of the Big Question: What does it mean to be a good citizen? Say: This text is about how people become citizens of a country. How do you think it will help you think about the Big Question? What do you think good citizens of a country do?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words freedom, important, and responsibility. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Say: When you have freedom, you can do what you want to do. Why is it important to have freedom? Why do you think people decide or choose to come to the United States? A responsibility is something you have to do. What is one responsibility that you have?

Oral Vocabulary

Some of the vocabulary in Becoming a Citizen includes words that might be new or challenging. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: When you make a promise, you agree to do something. What did Kartika’s parents promise to do?

WRITE/DRAW/DEFINE

Share or review how to make a three-column chart with columns labeled “Write,” “Draw,” and “Define.” Have students create their own chart on a piece of paper. Guide them to identify new words from the text and add them under “Write.” Help them add words or phrases to define each term under “Define,” and ask them to draw a picture under “Draw.” An entry for responsibility might look like this:

Write Draw Define
responsibility something you should do

During Reading

Elements of Informational Text

Text Structure Say: This text has a cause and effect structure. An effect is something that happens. A cause is why it happens. Direct students to this sentence on screen 6 (Printable Text p. x) “Some people come because they want a better life and more freedom.” Say: People want a better life and more freedom. This is the cause. The effect is that they come to the United States. Let’s read the next sentence. What is another reason for coming to the United States? (Possible response: to work) You may wish to create a list of causes and effects on the board. Point out the word because in the sentence. Ask students to look for other clue words about causes and effects in the text.

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Cause and Effect Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about cause and effect structure, and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “explains why something happens or what makes something happen”

Text Features Say: This text has headings. These are words at the beginning of a section that help tell what the section is about. Project the text or have students preview the first section. Ask: What is the heading of this part of the text? (Rights and Responsibilities)

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column write “Headings.” Work with the class to fill in the column with examples of how headings help readers understand the text. For example: “help show what the section is about”

After Reading

Vocabulary

After students have read Becoming a Citizen, have them review their Write/Draw/Define charts. Ask: Are there any other words you’d like to add?

Display images from Becoming a Citizen to help students review vocabulary words, such as freedom, responsibility, and promise.

Discuss Across Texts

Encourage students to make connections between Becoming a Citizen and other texts they have read on related subjects. You may wish to provide sentence frames to scaffold your discussion.

  • What other facts have you read about becoming a US citizen?
  • People can be citizens of a community and a country. What have you read about being a good citizen of your community?
  • In How to Be a Good Citizen, you learned about rights and responsibilities. What rights do U.S. citizens have? What responsibilities do citizens have?

Track the Texts After the discussion, have students return to the Good Citizenship: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in the Becoming a Citizen row with something they learned about the rights and responsibilities of US citizens.

Discuss The Big Question

Lead a class discussion around this text’s Big Question: What does it mean to be a good citizen? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • How can people be good citizens of the United States?
  • Kartika’s parents promised to follow the laws of the United States. Why is it important for citizens to follow the laws?

Write About the Big Question

Have students write and draw one right that citizens of the United States have. Then, ask them to write and draw one responsibility that citizens have. If you wish, have students work together as a class to brainstorm citizens’ rights and responsibilities, based on the text.

Good Citizenship



Wrap It Up

Now that the class has read some or all of the texts in this set, it is time to pull together all the information they have learned from their reading in order to explore and share their evolving understanding of the Big Question.

Vocabulary Round-Up

Have students review the Write/Draw/Define charts they have made, and invite them to play a game. Have volunteers stand up and say the definition or show their picture of a vocabulary word. Ask other students to guess the vocabulary word.

Discuss The Text Set’s Big Question

Guide a class discussion around the Big Question: What does it mean to be a good citizen? Encourage students to bring in knowledge they have learned from all the texts they have read in the text set, using as many of the words from their Write/Draw/Define charts as they can.

  • What have you learned about being a good citizen? Why is it important to be a good citizen?
  • What do good citizens do? What are some things you do that show good citizenship?
  • What did you already know about being a good citizen before you did this reading?
  • Which texts were the most helpful in answering the Big Question?

Culminating Project

Have students apply what they’ve learned from the text set to demonstrate and enhance their understanding. Review the Project Rubric with students, explaining in detail the expectations for the project.

PROJECT: A Plan to Help Your Community

Have students write a short informational text (3-5 sentences) describing a plan to help their community. They can draw pictures to illustrate the steps in their plan. Say: Think about a problem. What is a way that you can help to solve the problem? How would it help your school or neighborhood? Guide students to think about how friends, family, or teachers could help with their plan.

Some students may benefit from talking with another student to work out their ideas or to figure out what to draw or write. Remind them to review what they wrote in the Write About the Big Question to help them write. Encourage them to use vocabulary they learned from the texts in their writing.

Prepare to Share

Provide time for students to practice sharing their culminating project. If they are working with a partner, remind them to work collaboratively and follow agreed upon rules, norms, and protocols.

Use the bulleted points below to model good speaking and listening skills.

  • When I share my project, I look at the audience. I don't keep my eyes down.
  • As I speak, I make sure that everyone can hear what I am saying.
  • I pronounce each word clearly and correctly.
  • I do not rush, but speak clearly and at a natural pace.
  • I pay attention with my eyes and ears when others are speaking.

Celebrate!

Have students share their projects with the class. As students share, be sure to model and encourage strong listening skills.

Discussion Tips

An important part of building a vibrant classroom culture is creating an atmosphere in which all students feel empowered to participate in powerful classroom conversation. You may have noticed that your students who are naturally extroverted often participate freely in a whole-class discussion, while those who are more introverted or, perhaps, who come from cultures that respect different classroom norms, may need encouragement to share. You can use sentence prompts like these to invite all students into the conversation:

“That’s so interesting … please tell me more!”

“Can someone explain that in a different way?”

“What are some examples of that?”

“Can someone add on to that idea?”

Sharing and discussing ideas will often involve agreeing and/or disagreeing with what others say. As students listen carefully and respond thoughtfully to their classmates’ ideas, encourage them to express themselves in respectful and constructive ways. You may wish to post these sentence starters prominently, so students can refer to them often:

“I agree, and I also think fill in the blank line__________ .”

“I mostly agree, but fill in the blank line__________ .”

“I like what you just said because fill in the blank line__________ .”

“I respectfully disagree because fill in the blank line__________ .”
